What We Treat

Travel across borders for the purpose of better health and treatment.

Types of medical tourism
There are three types of medical tourism: outbound, inbound, and domestic or intrabound.

• Outbound: This type of medical tourism occurs when patients travel from the home country to a foreign country to receive medical treatment.
• Inbound: This type involves patients traveling from a foreign country to the home country in search of medical care.
• Intrabound or Domestic: Refers to patients traveling to another part of their own country. No international travel happens.

Range of medical treatment services
The medical tourism market is heavily skewed in favor of a few specific medical treatments. They include:

• Dental / Dentistry
• Orthopedics v
• Cosmetic / Plastic Surgery
• Complimentary and Alternative Medicine (CAM)
• Cardiology (Heart Surgery)
• Bariatric Surgery (Weight Loss Surgery)
• Stem Cell Therapy
• Fertility Treatment
• Eye Surgery (Ophthalmology)
• Ears, Nose and Throat (ENT)
• Organ Transplants
• Neurology
• General Diagnostics
• Rehabilitation

Factors affecting medical tourism
Medical tourism is significantly affected by a number of factors. Some of them include:

• Shared history: Travel patterns are clearly evident when one searches for it. Patients tend to go to nations which have a shared history with their own. For example, US and UK patients tend to go to commonwealth countries like India for treatment, while there is considerable cross-border medical travel among the former USSR states.

• Geographical factors: A major factor that influences medical tourists is the proximity of the geographical area where the patient will receive treatment. People prefer to go to countries adjacent to their own, as compared to going to a nation farther away.

• Demand and currency fluctuations: Demand for medical tourism may not be stable. The industry depends on a number of external as well as economic factors like varying consumer preferences and currency exchange rates. Fluctuations in currency values may make some destinations less attractive to consumers. Concerns about traveling to and the safety in certain parts of the world may force patients to explore other, more obscure markets. It is apparent that worsening economic conditions of the patients' originating countries affect medical tourism. For example, the sharp rise of US healthcare expenses have prompted an ever-increasing number of US citizens to travel abroad for medical care.

• Four drivers: An increase in medical tourism correlates to four main drivers - availability, quality, familiarity, and cost. Additionally, bio-ethical legislation gains relevance in some cases. Some treatments could be considered illegal in the patient's home country, but accepted as a legal procedure in the destination nation.

• Other factors: Better quality of treatment at the destination country, the number of specialists available, quicker treatment, less wait times and the opportunity to visit a foreign country and recuperate in a peaceful environment also play a major role in medical tourism.

The changing technological and economic conditions of the world-wide medical tourism industry have led to the creation of many opportunities for both patients and businesses.
